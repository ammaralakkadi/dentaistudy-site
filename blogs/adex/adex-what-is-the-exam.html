ADEX Exam 2025 – What Candidates Must Know

A clear, examiner-focused guide to the ADEX exam structure, scoring, and what you need to pass.

Quick Answers

What is the ADEX exam?

A national clinical dental licensure exam used by most U.S. states, assessing manikin skills and OSCE knowledge.

Who administers ADEX?

The Commission on Dental Competency Assessments (CDCA-WREB-CITA).

What are the main ADEX components?

Manikin-based clinical skills (Class II, Class III, provisional crown) and a computer-based OSCE.

Is ADEX accepted everywhere?

Nearly all U.S. states accept it, but always check your state board’s latest list.

Is ADEX hard?

It is predictable if you follow the scoring criteria — most failures come from avoidable technical errors.

How long should you prepare?

4–8 weeks of focused drilling for manikin skills plus consistent OSCE practice.

1. ADEX: the modern U.S. dental licensure exam

The ADEX exam was created to standardise clinical testing across states. Instead of regional exams with different rules, ADEX offers one unified pathway.

From 2020 onward, adoption accelerated — making ADEX the most widely accepted clinical licensure exam in the United States.

2. What exactly does ADEX test?

ADEX focuses on two major skill areas:

  • Manikin-based technical skills – precision, margins, anatomy, and avoiding critical errors.
  • OSCE decision-making – radiology, treatment planning, medical management, anesthesia, ethics, and clinical judgment.

ADEX major components

1. Class II preparation (mesio-occlusal or disto-occlusal)
2. Class III preparation
3. Provisional crown
4. OSCE scenarios (digital case-based exam)

3. Why candidates fail ADEX

The most common failing points are not “poor dentistry” — they are predictable technical mistakes.

  • Pulp exposure or over-reduction
  • Unsupported enamel
  • Margins not meeting criteria
  • Breaking the adjacent tooth
  • Inconsistent proximal box dimensions

The exam is strict, but not mysterious — if you follow the criteria, you pass.

4. Understanding the scoring system

ADEX uses calibrated examiners and defined cut-points for passing. Each task is graded under:

  • Critical errors – automatic failure
  • Standard errors – point deductions
  • Competency marks – meeting required criteria

Examples of automatic failures

● Pulp exposure
● Adjacent tooth damage
● Open gingival margin in Class II
● Incorrect tooth selection

Candidates who study the scoring criteria before touching a handpiece perform significantly better.

5. ADEX OSCE: the decision-making part

The OSCE tests how you think, not how fast you drill. Topics include:

  • Radiograph interpretation
  • Anesthesia selection
  • Medical emergency management
  • Treatment planning
  • Ethical/legal scenarios

Each question is based on real clinical decision patterns — not trick questions.

6. How long does ADEX preparation take?

A realistic timeline is:

  • 2–3 weeks – mastering manikin fundamentals
  • 2–3 weeks – refining preparations to pass criteria
  • 1–2 weeks – OSCE case practice & mock exams

Consistency beats intensity. Good candidates drill daily for short sessions instead of waiting for long study days.
